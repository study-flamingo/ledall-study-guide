#### THE REACTIVE MINDSET

**"Standing at Your Defence":**

Most techniques in the manuscript are proactive (you initiate). The Stopping Rabett is different:

**Patient Defense:**
- Don't rush to attack
- Wait for opponent's move
- Observe their pattern
- Identify opening
- Strike when they commit

**Why This Works:**
- Opponent thinks they have initiative
- They commit to their attack
- During commitment = vulnerable
- You interrupt = they can't recover
- Your timing defeats their technique

**Modern Application:**
- Counter-fighters use this approach
- Wait for opponent to attack
- Interrupt their preparation
- Works against aggressive opponents
- Frustrating for attacker

#### "BEPAYSTE" - THE DECEPTION

**Medieval English "Bepayste":**

**Meaning:**
- To deceive
- To trick
- To beguile
- To mislead

**How You Deceive:**
- Appear passive (standing at defense)
- Opponent thinks you're waiting/hesitant
- They believe they can attack safely
- They begin their attack
- You stop it = they were deceived

**Psychological Warfare:**
- Opponent's confidence shaken
- "I thought I had initiative"
- "How did they know I was attacking?"
- Creates hesitation
- Future attacks more tentative

#### TIMING: THE CRITICAL ELEMENT

**When to Execute Rabett:**

**Scenario 1: During Proffer**
- Opponent lifts sword (preparation)
- Opponent shifts weight (commitment)
- Opponent's eyes move to target (intention)
- **NOW** = execute rabett
- Before they complete proffer

**Scenario 2: During Chamber**
- Opponent proffers and rakes
- Opponent pulls sword back (chambering quarter)
- Sword at highest point (preparation)
- Hands furthest from you (extended)
- **NOW** = execute rabett
- During their chamber = most vulnerable

**Too Early:**
- They haven't committed
- They can adjust
- Rabett wastes energy

**Too Late:**
- Their attack already launched
- You're defending instead of interrupting
- Lost initiative

**Perfect Timing:**
- Between intention and execution
- They're committed but not launched
- Can't recover in time
- Your rabett stops them

#### TACTICAL APPLICATIONS

**Against Aggressive Opponents:**

**Their Pattern:**

**Your Response:**

#### THE SAME-LEG PATTERN

**Why Same Leg:**

**Speed:**
- Left leg in
- Left leg back
- One leg = one motion
- Faster than two-leg sequence

**Deception:**
- Unusual pattern
- Opponent expects right follow-up
- You're already gone
- Can't counter effectively

**Safety:**
- Minimal commitment
- Quick recovery
- Reduces exposure time
- Maximum efficiency

**Training Challenge:**
- Requires balance
- Tests coordination
- Develops unique skill
- Worth the difficulty

#### PARTNER DRILL: "THE INTERRUPT GAME"

**Setup:**
- Partner announces: "I will attack in 5 seconds"
- Partner executes proffer or proffer-rake-chamber
- You must stop them with rabett
- Timing is scored

**Scoring:**
- Stop during their preparation: 3 points (perfect)
- Stop during their execution: 1 point (adequate)
- Stop after they complete: 0 points (too late)
- Stop before they begin: -1 point (too early)

**Goal:**
Consistent 3-point scores. This means:
- Perfect timing recognition
- Instant execution
- Correct distance judgment
- True "stopping" capability

**Variation:**
- Partner doesn't announce
- Attacks randomly
- You must always be ready
- Builds constant vigilance

#### MINIMALIST PHILOSOPHY

**Why This Technique Is Simple:**

**Actions:**
1. Observe
2. Rabett (one action)
3. Void (recovery)
4. Stop guard

**Four total steps**â€”one of simplest in manuscript

**Design Intention:**
- Reactive techniques must be simple
- No time for complex sequences
- Instant response required
- Simplicity = reliability

**Principle:**
Defense should be simpler than offense. Attacker plans, defender reacts. Complex reactions fail under pressure. Simple reactions save lives.

George Silver: "The true defense is quick and simple. He who decorates his defense dies decorated."[7]

#### HISTORICAL CONTEXT: COUNTER-TIME

**Medieval Fencing Theory:**

**Three Times:**
1. **Opponent's Time:** They attack, you defend
2. **Your Time:** You attack, they defend
3. **Counter-Time:** You attack during their preparation

**Stopping Rabett:**
Perfect example of counter-time:
- You don't wait for their attack
- You don't attack independently
- You attack during their attack's preparation
- Counter-time = highest sophistication

**Why Rare in Manuscripts:**
Most sources teach proactive techniques. Counter-time requires:
- High skill
- Perfect timing
- Tactical patience
- Psychological strength

**The Stopping Rabett:**
Teaches all these qualities.

#### THE DRAGON'S TAIL MOTION

**"As the Sword Wavyth About":**

**Circular Overhead:**
- Sword circles above head
- Continuous motion
- Like helicopter rotor
- Creates defensive sphere
- Point describes circles

**Right Hand Control:**
- One-handed execution
- Right hand (rear hand) leads
- Requires strength and control
- Left hand free or guiding lightly

**Function:**

**Defensive:**
- Attacks from above = blocked by circle
- Creates space around head/body
- Opponent hesitates to enter
- "Waving sword" = psychological barrier

**Offensive Preparation:**
- Circles build momentum
- Rotational energy
- Transfers into thrusts
- Power from movement

**Visual Impact:**
- Impressive display
- Demonstrates control
- Intimidating
- But substance over showmanship

#### THE PENDANT GUARD

**What Is Pendant:**

From Latin *pendere* = to hang

**Position:**
- Sword hanging down
- Point low or behind body
- Handle above
- Relaxed but ready

**Types of Pendant:**
- **Front Pendant:** Point down in front
- **Side Pendant:** Point down to side
- **Rear Pendant:** Point down behind
- **Dragon's Tail Pendant:** Likely rear or side

**Tactical Use:**
- Rest position (conserves energy)
- Deceptive (hides intent)
- Loads gravity for rising cuts
- Transitions easily to thrusts

**In This Technique:**
Likely the position sword arrives at after circular motion before thrusts.

#### BOW-THRUST TECHNIQUE

**The Bow-Thrust Reappears:**

Appeared in Fourth Chase. Now in Dragon's Tail. What is it?

**Most Likely Interpretation:**

**Arcing Thrust:**
- Doesn't travel straight
- Curves around defense
- "Bow" describes arc trajectory
- Enters from unexpected angle

**Execution:**
1. First thrust to face (straight)
2. Opponent guards face
3. Bow-thrust arcs around guard
4. Hits belly from side/angle
5. Bypasses frontal defense

**Alternative: Half-Sword**
- Close-range technique
- Grip on blade
- Arms form bow shape
- Powerful controlled thrust

**Training:**
Practice both interpretations. Use what works against specific defenses.

#### TRAINING PROGRESSION

**Week 1: Overhead Circles**
- Right hand only

- Maintain smooth circles

**Week 2: Add Movement**
- Circle while stepping

**Week 3: Circle to Thrust Transition**
- Complete circles

- Use momentum from circles
- Smooth transition

**Week 4: High-Low Thrust Sequence**
- Face thrust (straight)
- Bow-thrust to belly (arcing)

- Build combination

**Week 5: Full Integration**
- Proffer-rake opening

- Thrust combination
- Safety sequence
- Complete technique

**Week 6+: One-Handed Strength**
- Increase circle duration
- Add resistance (heavier sword)
- Faster circles
- Build right-arm strength
- Develop stamina

#### HISTORICAL CONTEXT: CIRCULAR GUARDS

**Rotating Defenses:**

Multiple traditions use circular motions:

**Japanese *Jodan Kamae*:**
- Overhead guard with movement
- Sword moves to track opponent
- Circular patterns common

**European *Zornhut* Variations:**
- Wrath guard with motion
- Sword circles threateningly
- Psychological and practical

**Chinese *Bagua* Circular Walking:**
- Entire martial art based on circles
- Continuous circular motion
- Defense through movement

**Universal Principle:**
Circles are harder to penetrate than lines. Moving target harder to hit than static.
